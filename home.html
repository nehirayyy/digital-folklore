Secondary Research

This project bases itself heavily on Know Your Meme (KYM) "the internet's oldest and only public contextual meme archive" (Walker). KYM has been a central tool for meme studies in the past and present era. Like all archives, however, it has its limitations and idiosyncrasies.

The meme ecosystem generally and mostly lives and continues on online forums, social media platforms, and intimate corners of the web such as personal and group chats. To document the pulse and evolution of this process in real-time; using APIs would be outside of the parameters in deadline. Instead when I research the activity on KYM, I'm looking at what the creatoesof this culture and the researchers in this field are diving deeper into, what lasts longer on people's palettes. It's a 3rd degree look into a 2nd degree look into this culture.

Crowdsourced and Professionally Monitored

The participatory meme encyclopedia that is KYM is created by the submissions of the users, as well as professional editors & writers in the team who monitor and fact-check everything (Walker). This co-creation with the users and the monitors also means that they have to agree on the necessarily liberal definitions of "a meme", which is often a point of disputation in this dynamic. User Response to the Qualification System

This chart reveals that Confirmed memes drive much more engagement compared to the Submission and Deadpool categories.As demonstrated earlier in the Status Count graph, Confirmed memes can be considered rare in comparison to Submission memes. They even have their very own page on the archive. This points to the possibility that their rarity and spotlight may be the cause of their popularity. Memes that catch the attention of the moderators are likely already engaging, which could explain their higher view counts post-confirmation.
However, the Confirmed and Deadpool columns suggest that the views that a meme gets is tied to its intrinsic humor value rather than the mere fact of their confirmation by a moderation team.
On the other hand, "Deadpool" memes, marked as lacking in relevance or research, see far fewer views. This suggests that users pay attention to these quality markers when deciding which content to engage with.
Overall, the chart highlights the strong correlation between confirmation and increased meme engagement and not meme disqualification.
